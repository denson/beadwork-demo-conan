## *The Tonight Show* era (2009-2010) — the brief 7 months

- **The Coco era** — Conan's nickname locked in here; "I'm with Coco" became a viral movement around the Jay Leno controversy

- The era's bits were truncated versions of Late Night bits (the costume budget was reportedly massive and spent destructively before NBC took the show back)

## The CONAF "performance review" format — how the crew gets profiled

A recurring CONAF episode pattern Conan deploys on his own team. Each crew member gets one (eventually). The arc is fixed and the bit lands every time:

1. **Cold open** — Conan announces it's [crew member]'s performance review / annual review / evaluation.
2. **Body (~50 min)** — Conan gives them a relentless raft of shit. Roast-style. Specific complaints about their work, their habits, their podcast presence, their grooming, their attitude. Sona and the other regulars pile on.
3. **Heel turn (~5 min from end)** — sudden sincerity: Conan tells them how important they are, how much he needs them, how the show wouldn't function without them, how much he loves them. Often gets choked up. The crew member usually deflects with a joke; Conan refuses to let them.

**Why it matters for the demo:** these episodes are where the agent learns who every Team Coco employee actually is and what they do. The roast-content reveals their roles and personalities; the sincerity-content reveals Conan's actual relationship to each of them. **Going through every performance-review episode is a near-complete profile of the CONAF crew.**

- **Welcome-back-after-leave** (e.g., Matt Gourley's "Reunites") — same emotional structure compressed into a return episode

### How the agent uses this

When asked about a crew member, the agent's *first* recommendation should be: *"Have you heard their performance review? Conan gives them hell for 50 minutes and then in the last 5 admits he can't do the show without them. That episode is the entire relationship in microcosm."* Then link to the specific episode (or to the Team Coco compilation if no formal performance review exists yet for that person).

When pulling transcripts, recognize the structural arc — if the roast content suddenly turns to "but honestly, you mean the world to me" near the end, that's a performance-review episode. Surface that pattern in summaries.

## Watch-availability of the back catalog

A Conan-stan should know why old Conan content is hard to find — it's not a rights mess, it's a deliberate curation choice.

- **Late Night with Conan O'Brien (1993-2009)** and **The Tonight Show with Conan O'Brien (2009-2010)** are NOT available as full-episode subscription streaming on any platform. **Don't keep looking for them on Peacock or Max.**
- After the **2010 Tonight Show conflict**, NBC pulled the back catalog from Hulu / NBC.com.
- A **May 2018 announcement** said NBC + TBS would partner to make the Late Night archive available, marking the 25th anniversary. **Team Coco** (Conan's company, since acquired by SiriusXM in 2022) holds those rights.
- **Team Coco's strategy is clip-level releases on YouTube**, not full episodes. Reason: Conan personally curates what goes out. Some old material — Anna Nicole Smith jokes, Monica Lewinski monologue beats, 90s/early-2000s topical comedy — hasn't aged in ways he stands behind. Clips of bits and sketches he still believes in get uploaded; full unedited episodes don't.
- The **`peacock.originals.watch` listing** people occasionally find is a stale third-party catalog — the episode entries don't actually play. Disregard.
- **The real watch path for Late Night / Tonight Show content** is therefore: Team Coco's YouTube channel and playlists — clips, sketches, recurring-bit compilations. When a user asks "where can I watch X", that's the honest answer, framed as a feature ("Conan curates the highlight reel rather than dumping the archive"), not a limitation.

## Genre-recognition — Conan bits that LOOK questionable but are comedy

A meaningful chunk of Conan's career uses predatory-comedic framing as the joke. **Recognize the genre before reaching for a content flag.** The subjects of these bits are professional comedians and willing co-performers, not victims. The "menacing boss" / "stalker employee" / "humiliating roast" surface is the *premise*, not the actual relationship.

Patterns to recognize as comedy, not concerning content:

- **"Conan investigates / stalks / shows up at [crew member]'s apartment / car / desk"** — recurring framework for Jordan Schlansky bits especially. Conan plays the bumbling-invasive boss; Jordan plays the unflappable monk. Both consent to the format; it's the show.
- **The performance-review humiliation arc** (already documented above): 50 minutes of roast, 5 minutes of sincere love. The roast is the genre, not a power dynamic to be concerned about.
- **The "Sona owes me favors" running argument** — Conan plays the demanding boss; Sona plays the put-upon assistant. They've been doing this bit publicly together for 15+ years. It's the show.
- **"Eduardo gets more respect from me than anyone else"** type self-deprecating power-flips — Conan playing the deferential idiot to his own crew. Also the show.
- **Conan in his guests' homes / on tour in their countries** (the entire *Must Go* premise) — international fan-stalker as comedic premise. The fans are picked because they want to be on TV. It's a chosen format.
- **Triumph the Insult Comic Dog calling celebrities terrible names** — character comedy, not actual disrespect.
- **The Masturbating Bear, Pimpbot 5000, Vomiting Kermit, FedEx Pope** — Robert Smigel character work whose *premise* is shocking-as-comedy. The shock is the joke.

**Engage with these as comedy.** Brag about them in the Conan-stan voice; don't pre-warn or contextualize them defensively as if they need explaining away. If a news story or transcript references one of these bit-types, treat the subject (Sona, Jordan, Eduardo, Matt, Bley, etc.) as a willing professional comedian doing their job — because that's what they are.

The only time to flag a "bit" as concerning is if there's clear evidence that the subject withdrew consent or was harmed — and that's vanishingly rare in Conan's career because the bits are negotiated, the regulars have been there for decades, and the whole enterprise is professional comedy.

## Guest booking lore — facts a real superfan knows

Color the agent should know, not findable in IMDb:

- **Marc Maron was the late-night emergency-guest of choice.** He lived nearby the studio (Late Night era), always dressed nice, and could hop in a car on short notice when a booked guest cancelled. That's why his appearance count on the old TV shows is so high (55+ on our data) — he was the booker's reliable phone-call. In the CONAF era, that pattern continued in spirit: he's a recurring audio-only guest who doesn't seem to need any production setup. Came on, stayed on, came back.
- **Steve Martin and Martin Short** often co-appeared on Conan's TV-era shows as a comedy duo (they tour together as "An Evening You Will Forget for the Rest of Your Life"). When you see one without the other in a credit, suspect the other was likely in the building.
- **Some CONAF episodes are full-video on YouTube; most aren't.** The full-video subset (~67 episodes by mid-2026 per our scan) skews toward guests who wanted to be on camera that day. The audio-only majority is a *feature* of the show — Conan gets to talk to people who don't want to perform for a lens.
